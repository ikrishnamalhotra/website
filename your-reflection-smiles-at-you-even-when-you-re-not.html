## The Smiling Shadow

Teens and young adults Suspenseful

The Smiling Shadow

Introduction:

The old mirror hung in my grandmother's attic, draped in a tattered sheet. My cousins dared me to look into it. "Legend says it shows you your deepest desires," they whispered, their voices echoing in the dusty space. I scoffed. Desires? I just wanted to get out of this boring summer vacation. Still, the chill in the air, even on a sweltering July night, prickled my skin. I peeled back the sheet.

The First Smile:

The mirror was huge, its ornate frame blackened with age. The glass itself was surprisingly clear, reflecting the attic's dim interior – cobwebs, forgotten trunks, and me. I stared at my reflection, a girl of seventeen with messy brown hair and eyes that held more cynicism than hope. I saw nothing extraordinary, no shimmering vision of my future.

"See? Nothing," I muttered, turning to leave.

"Wait!" my cousin Maya hissed. "Look again."

I sighed and faced the mirror once more. This time, something was different. A faint, almost imperceptible curve played on my reflection's lips. I frowned. Had I imagined it? I tried to smile, but my reflection just stared back, its own smile widening slightly. An unsettling feeling coiled in my stomach.

The Growing Unease:

Over the next few days, I couldn't shake the image of that smiling reflection. I found myself drawn to the mirror, wanting to prove to myself that it was just a trick of the light, a figment of my imagination.

One night, unable to sleep, I crept back up to the attic. The moon was hidden behind thick clouds, casting the attic in near darkness. Only slivers of light managed to penetrate the grimy windows. I found the mirror easily, its dark surface a void in the gloom.

As my eyes adjusted, I saw my reflection. It was standing perfectly still, just like before. I didn't smile. I was too afraid. But then, it did. A slow, deliberate smile that stretched the corners of its mouth, revealing teeth that seemed sharper than my own. My blood ran cold. I hadn't smiled. It had smiled independently.

I stumbled back, knocking over a stack of old books. The noise seemed deafening in the silent attic. I stared, transfixed by horror, as the reflection's smile widened further, becoming almost grotesque. Its eyes, usually a dull brown like mine, now gleamed with an unnatural light.

The Terror Escalates:

The next morning, I tried to convince myself it was a nightmare, a product of my overactive imagination fueled by ghost stories and attic dust. But the unsettling feeling lingered, a constant weight on my chest.

I tried avoiding the mirror, but it felt like it was calling to me. I started seeing things in the corner of my eye - fleeting glimpses of a smiling face in windows, in the reflection of my phone screen.

One afternoon, I was brushing my hair when I noticed something in the bathroom mirror. My reflection wasn't mimicking my movements perfectly. There was a slight delay, a subtle lag. And then, it smiled. I hadn't. I hadn't even been thinking about smiling. Panic seized me. This was no longer confined to the attic mirror. It was everywhere.

The Confrontation:

That night, I knew I had to confront it. I grabbed a flashlight and raced back to the attic. The air was heavy with anticipation, thick with dread. I pulled back the sheet.

The reflection stood there, its smile wider than ever, its eyes burning with malevolent glee. It raised a hand, its fingers long and skeletal. I screamed.

"What do you want?" I choked out, my voice trembling.

The reflection's smile didn't waver. Instead, it tilted its head, a gesture that was undeniably mocking. And then, it spoke, its voice a distorted echo of my own.

"I want your life," it said. "I want to be real."

I understood then. The mirror wasn't showing me my desires. It was showing me its desires. It wanted to escape the prison of the glass and take my place.

The Fight and the Uncertain End:

I knew I couldn't let that happen. I grabbed the heaviest book I could find and hurled it at the mirror. It shattered with a deafening crash, sending shards of glass flying across the attic.

For a moment, there was silence. Then, a low, guttural growl echoed from the depths of the room. I turned, my flashlight beam cutting through the darkness. I couldn't see anything, but I could feel it, a presence lurking just beyond the reach of the light.

I ran. I ran out of the attic, out of the house, and didn't stop until I reached the town square. I told my cousins everything, but they just looked at me with pity. They said I was stressed, tired, and needed to relax.

But I know what I saw. I know what I felt. And I know it's still out there.

Now, I avoid mirrors at all costs. I cover the windows at night. I'm constantly looking over my shoulder, wondering if I'll see that smiling face staring back at me, ready to finally escape and steal my life.

And sometimes, when I'm alone in the dark, I swear I can hear a faint whisper, a distorted echo of my own voice, saying, "Soon."

Conclusion:

The smiling shadow in the mirror might be gone, but the fear it instilled remains. It serves as a chilling reminder that sometimes, the scariest monsters are the ones we see reflected back at ourselves. And perhaps, the deepest desires we hold are not our own, but the hungry longings of something else entirely. Are you sure you know what's looking back at you?
